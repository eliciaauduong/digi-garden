## Objective
Examine how companies collect, model, and exploit behavioural data while individuals have no meaningful visibility into what is held, how it is used, or what it predicts about them. Explore the structural power imbalance this creates, current regulatory approaches, and whether emerging models like Web3 can genuinely address it.

## Why this topic
Companies build narrow, static data portraits of individuals — a data point rather than a person — and use that portrait to predict and exploit behaviour. Over time, individuals have handed data to countless platforms and services with no way of knowing who holds what. The GDPR gives the right to request that data but not the means to understand it. That gap between access and comprehension is where the real power sits.

## Working thesis
Data asymmetry is both a knowledge problem and a power problem, but the second is the harder truth. Disclosure alone cannot fix the imbalance — even when consumers have legal access to their data, they lack the [[cognitive capacity|cognitive]] and technical tools to process it meaningfully. Comprehension at scale is impossible for an individual. This means the asymmetry is structurally self-reinforcing: the same cognitive load that makes data overwhelming to process is what allows companies to maintain the power imbalance behind a facade of transparency.

## Key tensions
Web3 and decentralised data ownership are frequently proposed as solutions, but they introduce their own complexity barriers — blockchain literacy, wallet management, and technical overhead may simply replace one inaccessibility with another. Also: companies argue that aggregated behavioural data produces genuinely useful products. At what point does that argument hold, and when does it become cover for exploitation?

## Open questions
- What are current data policies in Australia and globally, and how do they compare? 
- Which countries or companies are leading on closing the asymmetry? 
- What Web3 projects are actively working on data ownership and how viable are they? 
- What are the practical limitations consumers face in even attempting to reclaim their data? 
- How can consumers become meaningfully informed rather than just technically informed?
