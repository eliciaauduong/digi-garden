## Objective
Examine trust as a foundational layer — both epistemic (shared understanding of truth) and relational (built through repeated connection). Understand how information fragmentation and platform incentives that reward negativity are simultaneously eroding both layers, and what the consequences are for individuals and communities.

## Why this topic
Truth feels like a prerequisite for feeling safe in the world and in community. The erosion of shared truth and the rise of platform-driven distrust feels like a structural threat to the conditions that make genuine community possible. Loneliness is the downstream symptom, not the root cause.

## Working thesis
Trust operates on two distinct layers that are failing simultaneously. The epistemic layer — a shared foundation of truth — is fragmenting under algorithmic personalisation, AI-generated content, and the impossibility of verification at scale. The relational layer — trust built through repeated human connection — is eroding because platforms centre the individual over the common good and amplify conflict over care. Distrust then creates loneliness, and loneliness makes rebuilding trust harder, creating a self-reinforcing cycle with no natural exit point.

## Key tensions
Questioning everything is epistemically healthy but societally destabilising — a population that trusts nothing cannot coordinate on shared problems. Also: restoring trust may require platforms to deprioritise engagement, which conflicts directly with their business model. Who has incentive to break the cycle?

## Open questions
- What creates a [[cost of community|community built on trust]] — what are the actual conditions and mechanisms? 
- How can trust be restored once broken, and are there documented examples of recovery at scale? 
- What are the individual and community-level impacts of chronic distrust?
